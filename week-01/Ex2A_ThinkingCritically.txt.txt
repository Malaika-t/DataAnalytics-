------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------Question 5: Why did the line for Milkshake change? Hint: Click on the icon next to “Interest over time” for more information about how trend results are displayed.

Google trends displays search interest on a relative scale from 0 to 100.So,
100 = the peak interest for that time period 
50 = half the popularity 
0 = insufficient data 

The line for Milkshake changes because when Milkshake was only being tracked, Milkshake itself was the highest value, so it was near 100. But once Hamburger was added, it became the new peak, and Milkshake was then rescaled relative to the Hamburger's highest interest. That's why Milkshake line fell, the data didn't change only the reference point did.

Question 7: What does all of this suggest about the reliability of the conclusions reached in the article?

The article's conclusions are only partially reliable.
Milkshake and Breakup searches follow similar trend pattern, suggesting some connection exists.
The article only claims milkshakes topped 6 specific states not the whole country, so it could still be true regionally. 
The data covers only a year, too short to identify a long-term pattern.
Hamburger is searched more than Milkshake, if comfort food and breakups were linked then milkshake should rank higher. 
Correlation does not equal causation, similar search patterns do not prove breakups drive milkshake cravings.
Breakup isn't heavily searched, making it a weak basis for food preference conclusions.
The article uses only one data source, a reliable conclusion needs multiple data sources.
Google trends uses relative scaling not real search volumes.
