1. Start by reviewing the data.gov home page. What is this?

Data.gov is the U.S. government's open data portal. It serves as a centralized repository where federal, state, local, and tribal government agencies publish their datasets for public access. The site allows users to search, browse, and download a wide variety of government data across topics such as agriculture, climate, education, energy, finance, health, and more. The goal of Data.gov is to increase public access to high-value government data to encourage transparency, research, innovation, and civic engagement.

3. Find the Topic Category for Human Health. Review the range of data sources available for this topic. Why do you think these were tagged with the Human
   Health category?

"Topic Category for Human Health" returned 293 datasets.

These datasets were tagged with the Human Health category because they contain information that directly or indirectly affects people's physical wellbeing.
This includes data on disease rates, environmental exposures, food safety and nutrition, healthcare access, statistics, and public health programs. Even datasets such as agricultural or environmental data, are tagged as Human Health when their content has implications for how conditions in the environment affect human bodies.

7. Search for USDA, and click into the result. Within the USDA datasets, search for "food composition". Notice that the top three results are all datasets
   concerning nutrition information. Try searching the USDA page for "nutrition". How does that affect your results?
(a) One of the tricky things about working with data is that sometimes the terminology used by a data source is not obvious. You may need to dig
       around and be creative to find what you're looking for.

Searching "food composition" within USDA's datasets returned 17 datasets. The top results were nutrition-focused, including:
  Composition of Foods Raw, Processed, Prepared USDA National Nutrient Database for Standard Reference
  Key Foods
  FoodData Central
  
Switching the search term to "nutrition" dramatically expanded the results to 229 datasets. The results shifted to include broader topics like nutrition assistance programs, school meal programs, and nutrition policy reports, not just food composition databases. This shows how different keywords can surface very different datasets even when looking for similar information. "Food composition" is a more scientific term, while "nutrition" casts a much wider net and captures program-level and policy-level data as well.

8. Go back to the results for "food composition", and click on FoodData Central. Review the catalog page for this data source, then click on the button for
   Visit Page. Take some time to explore the FoodData Central website.
   
The FoodData Central catalog page describes it as a USDA food and nutrient data system that consolidates several databases (FNDDS, SR Legacy, Branded Food Products Database) into one platform.


(a) Try searching for "butter", or any of your favorite foods. What kind of results do you get? What happens when you click on a result?

When searching for "butter" on FoodData Central, the results are organized by data type. Under Foundation Foods, 5 results appeared:
  Almond butter, creamy (NDB# 12195) - Nut and Seed Products
  Butter, stick, salted (NDB# 1001) - Dairy and Egg Products
  Butter, stick, unsalted (NDB# 1145) - Dairy and Egg Products
  Peanut butter, creamy (NDB# 16098) - Legumes and Legume Products
  Sesame butter, creamy (NDB# 100271) - Nut and Seed Products

Additional results appear under SR Legacy Foods (104), Survey Foods/FNDDS (213), and Branded Foods (67,143 results). When you click on a result, you are taken to a detailed nutrient profile page for that specific food, showing values for calories, macronutrients, vitamins, minerals, and other components per 100g or per specified serving size.


(b) Go back to the home page. Find Foundation Foods, and click on Downloads. What kind of data can you find on this page? How big are these files? Where can you find more information on the data types available?

The Downloads page for Foundation Foods offers:

DATA TYPES AVAILABLE:
  Foundation Foods data (individual data type)
  Full Download of All Data Types (all FoodData Central data combined)

FILE FORMATS:
  JSON format
  CSV format
  MS Access format

FILE SIZES:
  Most recent releases range from about 1.5M (April 2019) to 3.3M (April 2025)
  Files are released approximately twice per year (April and October)
  The most recent available release is April 2025

FILE SIZES (All Data Types, zipped):
  These are much larger, ranging from 69M (December 2019) to 449M (October 2024)

For more information on the data types available, the FoodData Central website provides documentation links directly on the downloads page and search results pages. The "Foundation Foods" link on the search results page leads to detailed documentation. Additional documentation can also be found through
the FoodData Central FAQ, Search Tips, and Inventory and Update Log links listed in the left sidebar of the Food Search page.
